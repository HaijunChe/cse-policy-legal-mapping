CSE Policy & Legal Environment — Methodology Brief

UNFPA Data Analytics & Visualization Consultancy — Homework Assignment
Prepared by: Haijun Che | Date: 11 May 2026

1. Objective

This assignment maps the policy and legal environment for Comprehensive Sexuality Education (CSE) across 8 countries spanning Africa, Asia, and Latin America. The goal is to produce a clear, navigable visual product that surfaces patterns and gaps across three dimensions: (1) CSE integration in school curricula, (2) age of consent laws, and (3) the existence of GBV-related legislation.

2. Country Selection Rationale

Eight countries were selected to balance three criteria: regional representativeness (4 UN sub-regions, 3 continents), policy spectrum diversity (progressive to restrictive), and data availability (presence in UNESCO GEM Report CSE profiles or UNFPA programme documents).

3. Data Sources & Methods

  • CSE curriculum status: UNESCO GEM Report CSE Country Profiles (2023) and PEER Education Profiles platform — the most comprehensive publicly available source on CSE policy globally.
  • Age of consent: Cross-referenced national penal/criminal codes with ILGA World reports and ECPAT country profiles. Where federal systems create legal pluralism (e.g., Nigeria), this is noted explicitly rather than collapsed into a single number.
  • GBV legislation: UN Women Global Database on Violence Against Women and World Bank Women, Business and the Law 2024 dataset, supplemented with official national gazettes.
Transparency note: Every data point in the dashboard links to its primary source. Where the same country appears in multiple datasets with conflicting characterisations, the most recent authoritative source was prioritised and discrepancies are noted in the caveats field.

4. Key Insights

  • No country in this sample scores "No" on CSE. Even in restrictive environments (Indonesia, Ethiopia), some form of sexuality education exists — typically embedded in HIV/AIDS or health education rather than branded as CSE. This suggests that the framing debate ("CSE" vs. "life skills") is as significant as the presence/absence debate.
  • "Law on the books" does not equal "law in practice." South Africa has comprehensive GBV legislation but one of the world's highest femicide rates. Argentina's ESI Law is regionally progressive but teacher training gaps persist. The dashboard flags this explicitly through caveats.
  • Federal and decentralised systems create significant sub-national variation. Nigeria's VAPP Act is adopted by only ~15 states; Indonesia's CSE content varies by province; Argentina's ESI implementation quality differs by jurisdiction. National-level snapshots necessarily obscure this granularity.

5. Limitations

  • Comparability: "Age of consent" is defined differently across legal systems (some include marital exemptions, some do not; some use close-in-age provisions). A single number does not capture this complexity.
  • Sub-national variation: This exercise reports at the national level. For federal or highly decentralised systems, significant intra-country differences exist but are not visualised here.
  • Static snapshot: Legal landscapes change. The Philippines raised its age of consent from 12 to 16 in 2022; Indonesia enacted the TPKS Law in 2022. This dashboard reflects the state as of May 2026.
  • Self-reported data: Some CSE profiles rely on government self-reporting to UNESCO, which may not reflect classroom reality.

6. Tools Used

Data: Manual compilation from UNESCO, UN Women, World Bank, and national legislative databases. No proprietary datasets were used.

Dashboard: HTML5, CSS3, Vanilla JavaScript, Leaflet.js (mapping), OpenStreetMap tiles. No build tools or frameworks — designed for zero-dependency, fast load times, and easy maintainability.
