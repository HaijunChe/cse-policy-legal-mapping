CSE Policy & Legal Environment — Methodology Brief

Project: UNFPA Data Analytics & Visualization Consultancy — Homework Assignment
Author: Haijun Che  |  Date: 11 May 2026  |  Dashboard: haijunche.github.io/cse-policy-legal-mapping

§1 Objective & Scope

This assignment maps the policy and legal environment for Comprehensive Sexuality Education (CSE) across 8 countries spanning Africa, Asia, and Latin America. The output is an interactive, publicly accessible dashboard that surfaces patterns and gaps across four dimensions: (1) CSE integration in school curricula, (2) age of consent laws, (3) GBV-related legal frameworks, and (4) LGBTQ+ legal context.

The dashboard goes beyond the homework requirement of two enabling-environment dimensions by adding a third (LGBTQ+) to provide a fuller view of the rights-based context in which CSE programmes operate.

§3 Dimensions & Scoring

  1. CSE in Education Systems — Yes / Partial / No, plus local naming (LSE, FLHE, LO, PKR, ESI, etc.).
  2. Age of Consent Laws — Numerical threshold, with notes on federal/dual systems (e.g., Nigeria).
  3. GBV-Related Laws — Yes / Partial / No, with primary legislation cited.
  4. LGBTQ+ Legal Context — Protected / Tolerated / Criminalized, with key legal instruments.

Scoring criteria. Yes Explicit national mandate with policy guidance. Partial Exists but not branded as CSE, or implementation uneven. No No national-level integration.

  7. National legislation and government gazettes (cited per country in the dashboard).

§5 Key Insights

  1. The Naming Gap. 0 / 8 score "No" on sexuality education — even restrictive contexts (Indonesia, Ethiopia) have curricula. Only 3 / 8 explicitly use the term "CSE". The challenge is framing, not existence.
  2. Latin America Leads. Argentina (ESI 2006) and Colombia (since 1994) have the longest CSE mandates. Both also protect LGBTQ+ rights — CSE thrives where broader rights are protected.
  3. Law ≠ Practice. South Africa has the strongest GBV legal framework on the continent — yet GBV prevalence is among the world's highest. Strong laws are necessary but not sufficient.
  4. Federalism Fragments. Nigeria's VAPP Act is adopted by ~15 of 36 states; Indonesia's CSE varies by province. National data masks sub-national fragmentation.
  5. 2022 Watershed Year. Philippines raised consent (12→16); Indonesia enacted TPKS Sexual Violence Law. Both followed decades of advocacy.
Implication for UNFPA. Programming should distinguish: (i) existence interventions for low-CSE contexts; (ii) quality/branding interventions for partial contexts (transition embedded curricula to explicit CSE framework); (iii) implementation monitoring for high-CSE contexts where law-practice gaps persist. The Out-of-School CSE programme is well-positioned to address gaps formal systems cannot reach.

§6 Limitations

  1. Law ≠ practice gap. South Africa has comprehensive GBV laws but among the world's highest GBV prevalence.
  2. Sub-national variation. Nigeria's VAPP Act adopted by only ~15 of 36 states; some northern states use Sharia-based alternatives.
  3. Definitional comparability. Argentina's age of consent is 13 but with a critical "no abuse" condition for 13–15. The raw number misses nuance.
  4. Static snapshot. Reflects May 2026. Philippines 2022 reform and Indonesia 2022 TPKS Law are recent examples of fast change.
  5. Self-reported data. CSE profiles partly depend on government reporting to UNESCO; classroom-level reality may differ. Cross-verification with civil society recommended.

§7 Tools Used

Data: Manual compilation from public sources (no proprietary datasets). Stack: HTML5, CSS3, Vanilla JavaScript, Plotly.js (choropleth, donut, bar charts), Leaflet.js (mini country maps), OpenStreetMap (tiles), Lucide icons. Hosting: GitHub Pages (static). Design principle: zero build step, no framework — fast load, easy maintenance, full transparency of source.
