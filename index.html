Methodology

Methodology & Limitations

Scope, sources, scoring, and limitations.

§1

Country Selection

Eight countries (n=8) selected to maximize variance across three criteria: (i) regional coverage — 4 UN sub-regions across 3 continents; (ii) policy spectrum — from explicit CSE mandate (Argentina, Colombia, Philippines) to embedded curricula (Kenya, Nigeria) to restrictive contexts (Indonesia, Ethiopia); (iii) UNFPA portfolio relevance — Ethiopia (Phase I pilot) and Nigeria (Phase II) are out-of-school CSE focus countries.

§2

Dimensions

  1. CSE in Education Systems — integration status (Yes/Partial/No) and local naming convention.
  2. Age of Consent Laws — legal threshold (numerical, with notes on federal complexity).
  3. GBV-Related Laws — comprehensive framework status (Yes/Partial/No) with primary legislation cited.
  4. LGBTQ+ Legal Context — protection level (Protected/Tolerated/Criminalized) with key legal instruments.
§3

Scoring Criteria

Yes

Explicit national mandate with policy guidance.

Partial

Exists but not branded as CSE, or implementation uneven.

No

No national-level integration in curricula.

§4

Data Sources

  1. UNESCO GEM Report (2023). CSE Country Profiles. — unesdoc.unesco.org
  2. PEER Education Profiles. — education-profiles.org
  3. UN Women Global Database on VAW (2024). — evaw-global-database.unwomen.org
  4. World Bank (2024). Women, Business and the Law. — wbl.worldbank.org
  5. ILGA World (2023). State-Sponsored Homophobia Report. — ilga.org
  6. UNFPA Programme Documents. — unfpa.org
  7. National legislation and government gazettes (cited per country in detail panels).
§5

Limitations

  1. Law ≠ practice. Legal existence does not guarantee enforcement (e.g., South Africa has the strongest GBV framework on the continent but among the world’s highest GBV prevalence).
  2. Sub-national variation. Federal systems mask intra-country differences (e.g., Nigeria’s VAPP Act adopted by ~15 of 36 states).
  3. Definitional comparability. Terms vary across jurisdictions (e.g., Argentina’s age of consent is 13 but with “no abuse” conditions).
  4. Static snapshot. Reflects May 2026 state; rapidly evolving (e.g., Philippines 2022 reform; Indonesia 2022 TPKS Law).
  5. Self-reported data. CSE profiles partly depend on government reporting to UNESCO, which may not reflect classroom reality.
